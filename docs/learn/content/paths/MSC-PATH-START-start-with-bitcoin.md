# Start With Bitcoin

## 1. Introductory deck

Build a calm first model of Bitcoin through fundamentals, history, wallets, custody, keys, transactions, confirmations, everyday practices, and a final walkthrough showing how one transaction moves through the network.

## 2. Full destination copy

Bitcoin can feel difficult at first because one word often points to several different layers. Bitcoin is a network and protocol. bitcoin is the asset recorded through transaction outputs. A wallet manages keys and helps construct transactions, but it does not hold coins inside an application account. A confirmation records selected-chain inclusion, but it does not make every real-world decision safe or irreversible.

This path builds those distinctions gradually.

It begins with what Bitcoin is, why people consider it useful, how the project developed, and what can be established about Satoshi Nakamoto. It then moves into wallets, custody, backups, keys, sending, receiving, fees, confirmations, and everyday operating habits. The final featured route connects those foundations into one complete transaction journey.

The path is welcoming by design. Readers are not expected to operate a node, understand mining hardware, or study consensus activation before learning the basic model. Advanced branches remain available later.

### Stage 1: What Bitcoin is

The first guide defines Bitcoin as an open monetary network and asset governed by independently verified consensus rules. It separates the network, the asset, the ledger history, wallets, miners, nodes, and rules so later guides have stable language.

The second guide asks why the system matters without turning technical properties into an investment promise. It examines direct digital ownership, open participation, predictable issuance rules, cross-border transfer, verification, and the tradeoffs that come with custody, privacy, volatility, and operational responsibility.

History comes next because Bitcoin did not appear as one finished product. The timeline connects earlier digital-cash research, the white paper, the genesis block, early software, market infrastructure, protocol changes, and recurring debates. It also teaches readers to separate documented events from mythology.

The Satoshi guide closes the stage by focusing on primary evidence. It explains what the pseudonymous creator published and built while refusing to present unverified identity claims as fact. The important transition is from a founder-led launch to an open project operated by many independent participants.

After this stage, readers should have a first mental model of Bitcoin without assuming that one person, company, or repository owns the network. Readers who want to continue from the historical timeline into people, companies, markets, and community can take the planned MSC-PATH-ECOSYSTEM | Explore the Ecosystem branch after the history portion.

### Stage 2: Ownership and custody

The wallet introduction begins the practical side of the path. A wallet manages keys, constructs transactions, and helps users interact with Bitcoin. It can be custodial or self-custodial, online or offline, simple or multi-party. The interface is not the protocol ledger.

Self-custody then asks who controls the keys needed to spend. Direct control can reduce dependence on a service, but it also creates responsibility for backups, devices, software, recovery, privacy, and continuity. The guide does not prescribe one arrangement for every person.

Seed phrases come next because many wallet recovery systems use a human-readable backup representation. A seed phrase is not an ordinary password and should not be entered into websites, support chats, cloud documents, screenshots, or unverified applications. Compatibility depends on the wallet standard and surrounding configuration.

Public and private keys complete the ownership stage. Private keys authorize spending. Public keys and signatures let nodes verify authorization without receiving the secret. Addresses, keys, scripts, signatures, and wallet balances remain related but different.

Readers who need a deeper custody and operational-safety sequence can branch to MSC-PATH-SAFE | Use Bitcoin Safely after the wallet introduction. That planned branch does not guarantee safety. It provides a more detailed framework for threats, recovery, privacy, and setup maintenance.

### Stage 3: Using Bitcoin

The sending and receiving guide turns the wallet model into a calm operational sequence. It covers destination verification, network and payment-path awareness, amount and fee review, signing boundaries, broadcast, pending states, and confirmation decisions. It avoids treating QR codes, wallet notifications, or transaction identifiers as guarantees.

The transaction and fee guide then explains inputs, outputs, change, transaction weight, fee rate, local policy, replacement, and mining incentives. Fees compete for limited block space, but one estimate cannot reserve a future confirmation time.

Confirmations appear after transactions and fees so readers understand what is being confirmed. The first confirmation begins when a valid transaction appears in a block on a node's selected chain. Additional blocks increase depth. Reorganizations remain possible, and no universal confirmation count fits every payment or delivery risk.

Bitcoin Best Practices for Safe Everyday Use collects the practical boundaries into maintainable habits. It emphasizes software source, transaction review, recovery information, secret handling, wallet and node roles, UTXO effects, privacy limits, support scams, updates, and continuity. A best practice reduces avoidable risk while remaining appropriate to the user, tool, and threat model.

Readers who want the deeper network explanation can branch to MSC-PATH-NETWORK | Understand the Network after transactions and fees. That route follows UTXOs, local mempools, blocks, nodes, mining, proof of work, consensus, and upgrades.

### Stage 4: See the complete transaction journey

MSC-ROUTE-001 | How a Bitcoin Transaction Moves is the final synthesis step.

The route begins with wallet-selected UTXOs and new recipient and change outputs. It follows signatures, local checks, peer relay, node-local mempools, miner selection, candidate blocks, proof of work, independent node validation, accumulated-work chain selection, confirmation depth, and UTXO-set updates.

The purpose is not to introduce every advanced detail. It is to connect the beginner concepts into one coherent journey and show which system performs each job.

By the end of the path, a reader should be able to explain several basic distinctions:

- Bitcoin is not one company, server, or wallet application.
- A wallet manages keys and transaction activity rather than storing protocol coins in an account row.
- Custody describes control over spending authority.
- A seed phrase can represent powerful recovery material under a wallet standard.
- A transaction spends existing outputs and creates new outputs.
- Broadcast does not guarantee universal relay or confirmation.
- The first confirmation begins with selected-chain block inclusion.
- Everyday safety depends on the complete setup and maintained practices, not on one product or slogan.

Completing the path does not make someone financially, operationally, or technically safe. It provides a clearer model for asking better questions, recognizing system boundaries, and continuing into more focused learning.

The path is educational sequencing. Each guide and route remains a permanent canonical destination with its own navigation. Planned actions remain inactive until publication and confirmed URLs.

## 5. Key Terms

- **Bitcoin:** An open monetary network and asset governed by independently verified consensus rules.
- **Wallet:** Software or hardware that manages keys, constructs transactions, and helps users interact with Bitcoin.
- **Custody:** Control over the keys or authorization needed to move bitcoin.
- **Self-Custody:** Direct control of the keys needed to spend bitcoin.
- **Seed phrase:** A human-readable backup representation used by many wallets to derive keys under a wallet standard.
- **Private key:** Secret cryptographic material used to authorize spending.
- **Public key:** Cryptographic data derived from a private key and used in Bitcoin spending conditions and signatures.
- **Digital signature:** Cryptographic proof that a valid private key authorized a transaction or message.
- **Transaction:** A data structure that spends existing UTXOs and creates new outputs.
- **Address:** A destination representation used by wallets to construct a Bitcoin locking script.
- **Confirmation:** The inclusion of a transaction in a block, followed by additional blocks that increase settlement depth.
- **Best practice:** A generally recommended method that reduces avoidable risk while remaining appropriate to the user, tool, and threat model.
